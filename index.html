The App Store Accountability Act

07
The Bill

App Store Accountability Act

Senator Mike Lee (R-UT) and Representative John James (R-MI) introduced the App Store Accountability Act in May 2025. The Act treats the app store, rather than each individual app, as the chokepoint for children's online safety.5,12

It reaches stores with at least five million U.S. users and takes effect one year after passage.12

The Bill

Enforcement and Application

Preemption (§7)

The Act overrides state app-store laws, so developers follow one federal standard instead of a patchwork across Utah, Texas, Louisiana, and others.

FTC Enforcement (§6)

The Federal Trade Commission serves as primary enforcer. State attorneys general keep a secondary enforcement role.

Safe Harbor & Advisory (§5)

Developers who rely in good faith on store-provided age and consent signals gain protection. An advisory committee recommends age-rating practices.

Data minimization (§3(a)(6))

Stores may collect verification data only to confirm age, obtain consent, or keep compliance records, and must encrypt it.

Status in Congress

The House Energy and Commerce Committee advanced the bill on March 5, 2026, by a party-line vote of 26 to 23. It now awaits a full House vote.76

Congress.gov H.R.3149 §§3, 5–7 · 76. House Energy & Commerce Committee markup recap (3/5/26)
10
The Bill

Obligations

App Store Obligations (§3)
  • Request age information and verify each user's age category at account creation, using a commercially reasonable method.
  • Affiliate a minor's account with a parental account, then obtain verifiable parental consent before any download or purchase.
  • Provide the age category and consent status to developers, and notify them when a parent revokes consent.
App Developer Obligations (§4)
  • Verify the user's age category through the store's API before download, and confirm parental consent for minors.
  • Notify the store of any significant change to the app.

Part Three

Potential Ramifications

Many agree that minors face real harm online that, left unchecked, can lead to serious consequences. Those who oppose the bill maintain that it raises First Amendment concerns and imposes too great a liability.

12
Ramifications

The First Amendment Question

In McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Commission, 514 U.S. 334 (1995), the Supreme Court held that the First Amendment protects an author's choice to speak anonymously, and that a law burdening that choice must survive exacting scrutiny.68 Age verification tests whether checking age stays separate from checking identity.

  • A verifier can combine age data with public or leaked records to identify the user.
  • When a download traces back to a person, that person may avoid lawful but sensitive content, which the courts call a chilling effect.
  • That chilling-effect argument already defeated several state age-verification laws.14
14. AAF / Westling (5/7/25) · 68. McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Comm'n, 514 U.S. 334 (1995)
13
Ramifications

Support and Opposition

Supporters
  • Targeting stores imposes the least burden for age checks at scale.
  • Modern age-assurance confirms a category without an ID.
  • The rule applies to every store alike, so it reads as content-neutral.
  • It rests on contract law, not on speech.
  • Meta, X, Snap, and Pinterest endorse it; 88% of parents agree.
Opposition
  • It forces every user, adults included, to surrender anonymity.
  • Verification data invites breach, sale, or misuse.
  • It burdens a First Amendment right to anonymous access.
  • Courts already struck down comparable state laws.
  • Apple and Google oppose a duty to verify every user.
Apple

Warns that stores would have to collect and keep sensitive ID data for "every" user, even someone downloading a weather or sports app.64

Google

Called an equivalent state bill "one of the most extreme age-verification regimes" its policy team had seen.64

64. Yoti, quoting Apple & Google statements (7/1/25) · AAF / Westling (5/7/25)

Possible Solutions

Possible Solutions

A court strikes a law when a less-restrictive option exists. So the strongest fixes narrow the bill, protect the data it collects, and prove the mandate is necessary.

01 · Privacy by Design

Require methods that confirm an age category without revealing identity, such as zero-knowledge proofs or on-device biometric age estimation. The store returns only a yes-or-no age signal, so it never collects a government ID or birthdate, which is the data point critics fear will be breached or misused.

02 · Data Guardrails

Cap how long a verifier may keep any data, ban its resale or reuse for advertising, mandate industry-standard encryption, and require deletion once verification finishes. Real per-violation penalties give the limits teeth and narrow the bill's reach to its stated purpose.

03 · Prove Necessity

Build a legislative record showing why existing Apple and Google parental controls fall short of the goal. Strict scrutiny turns on necessity and narrow tailoring, so the sponsors must demonstrate that no less-restrictive tool already achieves what the mandate seeks.

15

Conclusion

01
The shift is structural. The Act moves responsibility from parents and individual apps onto the store, a new model of platform governance.
02
The harm convinces; the remedy divides. The Snap and TikTok litigation shows why lawmakers want to act, while the First Amendment remains the strongest objection to how the bill verifies age in an app store.
03
The courts will decide its fate. Because judges have already struck down comparable state laws, the same First Amendment challenge may determine whether this bill survives.
